Reformatted from BPS Reprographic Services -- Glossary (http://www.resources.com/bps/tiptools/glossary.htm)

Acetate:
Short name for cellulose acetate: a synthetic transparent material similar to celluloid but not easily flammable.
Available in different grades of thickness and color, with smooth or matte surface.
Used as a photographic base. Can be used for overlays.

Aperture Cord:
A card with rectangular holes, specifically prepared for the mounting or insertion of microfilm.

ASCII (Ask-ee):
The American Standard Code for Information Interchange, a system for referring to letters,
numbers and symbols by code numbers. Also known as text files.

Azo Dye:
Dye produced in the reaction of a diazonium compound with a coupler,
usually in the presence of ammonia, as in diazo reproduction.

Background:
Density, discoloration, or tint noticed in the fully exposed areas of a print above the color and
density of the base material. Often can be controlled in reproduction.

Base Color:
Tint to which a base stock is dyed during its manufacture.

Base Stock:
Any paper or material, such as film or cloth, to which a coating is applied.

Baud Raft:
Speed of data transmission - by modem, for example - usually expressed in bits per second (BPS).
Most common baud rates: 14,400, 28,800 and 36,600 bps.

BBS:
Short for bulletin board service, more commonly referred as GroupWare or online service.
A computer dedicated to maintaining messages and software and making them available over phone lines.
Users upload (contribute) or download (gather) messages and files by calling the BBS from their computers.

Bit:
The smallest possible unit of digital information. Short for binary digit, it can represent only 0 or 1, "off' or "on'.

Bit Map: Image made of dots.

Bit-Mapped Font:
A font made of a pattern (map) of dots, which requires different maps to look right in different sizes.

Bit-Mapped Graphic:
A picture or other graphic made of dots rather than of objects. Typically produced by paint programs.

Blocking Out:
The use of black or orange paper, photographic opaque, tape or india ink to fill in areas
on a negative so that they will not be reproduced on the positive print.

Blowback:
General term for an enlargement from a reduced size camera negative.

Blueline: See Diazo Brownlines: See Diazo

Byte:
8 bits. Bytes are typically used to represent characters in text.

CAD:
Computer-Aided Drafting, usually in areas of engineering, product design,
and architecture. Refers to both hard- and software.

Card Image:
A card containing an image on exposed and processed microfilm, which is secured in an aperture
in the card. The exposed and processed output of a mounter, a card-to-card printer,
a roll-to-card printer or a processor camera.

Clear Film Positive:
A 4 mil thick photographic film with no drafting surface (matte). Also known as "slick."

CMYK
Subtractive primaries cyan, magenta, yellow, black.

Color Model:
A color measurement scale or system that numerically specifies the perceived attributes of color.
Used in computer graphics applications and by color measurement instruments.

Colorimeter:
An optical measurement instrument that responds to color in a manner similar to the human eye
by filtering reflected light into its dominant regions of red, green, and blue.

Color Separation:
The conversion of the red, green, and blue color information used by a computer into cyan,
magenta, yellow, and black channels used in the printing process.

Color Transparency:
A film positive which portrays the subject in natural colors and is generally viewed by transmitted light.

COM (Computer Output Microfilm):
Microfilm containing data produced by a recorder from computer generated electrical signals.

Comb Bind:
A binding system using 19 rectangular punched holes and a plastic spine
which allows a document to open and lie flat.

Composite Printing:
A combination of multiple exposures onto photographic material using more than one negative.

Compression:
Reducing file sizes for speedy transmission or compact disk space.

Contact Print:
Print produced by exposure in immediate contact with the original or negative.
These prints are always the same size as the original or negative.

Continuous Tone Negative:
Used in reproduction of originals which encompass full gray scale.

Contrast:
Difference of tone between the darker and the lighter parts of an image.

Copy Negative:
A photographic negative made as an intermediate from which to produce prints.

d-Print:
Trademark name for digital xerographic large format plotting and printing.

Densitometer:
Device to measure the optical density of an image or base by measuring
the amount of incidental light reflected or transmitted.

Density:
Defined as the logarithm of the opacity in general terms, the relative darkness of an image area.

Developer:
Converts invisible latent image to visible form, reduces sensitized silver halide to metallic silver (black).

Diazo Paper:
A reproduction paper which depends on the light-sensitivity of diazonium compounds to form an image
when developed by means of either aqueous ammonia, heat or a specialized developing solution.

Diazo Prints:
A reproduction made using the diazo process. Also referred to as whiteprint, blueline, blackline, or sepia.

Diazo Process:
A reproduction method using material coated with a light-sensitive diazo compound that,
after exposure is subjected to ammonia-vapor or moist development to produce a colored,
positive dye image. A positive translucent or transparent original produces a positive image.

Diazo Vellum:
An intermediate on pre-transparentized paper, sensitized with a black or sepia diazo solution.
Latter most popular. See Sepia Intermed49tes.

Diffusion Transfer:
A photographic method involving image transfer. A sheet of sensitized material is exposed to
an image in a contact-printing frame or process camera and processed in contact with a receiver sheet.
The image is transferred during processing to the receiver sheet. When peeled apart, the image on
the receiver sheet will carry a posi~ five image. The same applies for a negative image.

Dimensional Stability:
Term applied to the relative ability Of photographic materials
to maintain their size and shape before, during and after processing.

Direct Positive:
Positive to positive, or negative to negative, reproduction without intermediate steps.

Dot: Another name for a pixel, the smallest image unit.

Dots Per Inch (DPI):
Measure of screen or printer resolution; the number of dots in one square inch.

Driver:
Software that tells a computer how to run an outside device, typically a printer, or that emulates doing so.

Eight-bit Color:
Said of images where each pixel has eight bits of memory assigned to it.
Produces a palette of 256 colors or grays.

Electrostatic Process:
Reproduction method in which image formation depends on electrical,
rather than chemical, changes induced by light.

Encapsulated PostScript (EPS):
A standard graphics format that consists of the PostScript code that tells the printer
how to print the image and a PICT image that tells the screen how to display it.

Eradicator:
A one, two, or three solution preparation used for making changes on intermediates and prints.
Specific preparations are required for photographic or diazo intermediates.

Fading: Loss of density in an image.

Film Negative:
See negative. Halftone Negative - See halftone, converts continuous tone original into a dot matrix
suitable for use in offset printing or photographic reproduction onto high contrast paper (HCP).
At BPS available screens: 65, 85, 100, 120, 133, 150, 200 and 300 lines per inch (Ipi).
Special screens (see code 6019) available: mezzotint 75, 150 Ipi;
grain or steel engraved 60 Ipi; straight or wavy line 100 Ipi.

Film Positive: A positive reproduction made on photographic film.

Fixed Line Mylar:
A 4 mil photographic film with drafting surface (matte) on two sides;
requires a chemical eradicator solution to remove image.

Foam Core: A foam filled presentation board used for mounting prints.

Font:
A collection of letters, numbers, punctuation marks and symbols with an identifiable
and consistent look; a typeface in all sizes and styles.

Font Family:
A term that is sometimes used to refer to all the styles and weights (bold, semibold, etc.) of a font.

Format:
The file structure that a document is saved in (e.g. ASCII, PICT, EPS, TIFF).
Most applications can save documents in one or more standard formats as well as their native format.

Gamut:
The range of different colors that can be interpreted by a color model or generated by a specific device.

Gateway:
One of the most intelligent connections between networks
(it translates between different network protocols).

Gigabyte ("Gig"):
A measure of computer memory or disk space that's equal to approximately
1000 megabytes, or about 179 million words.

Gatorboard:
A foam filled presentation board with a hardened durable surface used for mounting prints.

Generation:
Each succeeding stage in photographic or xerographic reproduction from original copy.

Gradient:
A smooth and even shift from one color or shade to another, through a continuum of shades or colors.

Grayscale:
Said of images that contain shades of gray as well as black and white, and of monochrome monitors
that can display grays, rather than black and white.

Halftone:
The reproduction of a photograph in which the gradation of tone is reproduced by various size dots
and intermittent white spaces. It is produced photographically by interposing a screen between the lens
and the film or directly by scanning a continuous line photograph and converting the image to a series of dots.

Hard Copy:
Printed copy, usually on paper or other nontransparent base.
An enlarged print made from microfilm.

HCP- See High Contrast Print.

High Contrast Print:
Also known as HCP, KP-5, TP-5, KC5; positive or reverse field (negative, TPP-5) image from line,
halftone or panchromatic negative. Available in matte or glossy finish.

Hue:
The basic color of an object, such as "red," "green," "purple," etc.
Defined by its angular position in a cylindrical color space, or on a Color Wheel.

Image: A likeness of the subject matter which appears on the reproduced copy.

Imagesetter:
A high-resolution digital phototypesetting machine capable of producing graphic images
and type onto photographic paper or film.

Inkjet Printer: A printer that forms characters and images with tiny jets of ink.

Intermediate:
A transparent or translucent reproduction made from an original drawing
and used in its place for making direct copies.

Kerning:
Closing up the space between certain letter pairs (like AV or To) to make them look better.
Increasing the space between letters is usually called letter-spacing.

Kilobyte:
A measure of computer memory or disk space that's equal
to approximately 1000 characters, or 170 words. Abbreviated k.

Laminate: Material made of two or more layers pressed together with an adhesive.

Landscape:
Printing sideways on a page, so that the longer sides are at the top and bottom. Compare to portrait.

Leading (Loh-ding):
The amount of space from the baseline of one line of type to the baseline of the next.
Usually measured in points.

Line Copy: Originals, containing only lines and solids and with no intermediate tone.

LPI:
Lines per inch. This setting is sometimes more important than DPI.
Correct LPI settings will enhance the printing of images.

Master: Original from which reproductions are made.

Matte:
In reproduction, a relatively rough, low-gloss surface particularly suited to drafting in either pencil or ink.

Megabyte ("Meg"):
A measure of computer memory or disk space equal to about 1OOO K, or about 175,000 words.

Microfiche:
Transparent film approximately 4"x 6" containing micro images in a group pattern
with a title heading large enough to be read by the naked eye.

Microfilm:
(1) Fine-grain, high resolution film containing an image greatly reduced from original.
(2) Recording microphotographs on film. (3) Raw film with characteristics of (1).

Modem:
A device that lets computers talk to each other over phone lines; short for modulator-demodulator.

Mounting:
Adhering a drawing or rendering, either to rigid material for display purposes,
or on cloth to add protection and longevity.

Mylar: A polyester film used in photographic and xerographic reproduction. See wash-off.

NCR: No carbon required, a type of special paper used for multipart forms.

OCR:
Optical character recognition - the ability of software and/or hardware to read text from paper.
Unlike a regular scanner that reads text (and everything else) as a series of dots, OCR scanners
and software recognize the characters and thus generate editable ("Smart") text.

Offset Printing:
Indirect photolithography in which the ink image is transferred to a rubber blanket
which in turn offsets the image onto a sheet of paper or other material.

Outline Font:
A font that is made up of an outline of the shape of each letter and that can be
scaled to any size without degradation of quality. See also PostScript font and bitmapped font.

Opacity:
(a) Photographic: blocking light transmission. (b) Paper: that property of a sheet
which prevents dark objects from being seen through the sheet.

Opaque Copy:
Material impervious to transmitted light which consequently must be reproduced by reflected light.

Overlay:
Transparent or translucent prints which, when placed one on the other, form a composite picture.

Paint:
A standard graphics format for low-resolution (72 dpi) bit-mapped images, sometimes abbreviated PNTG.

Panchromatic Negative:
Used for black and white reproduction of a color line original from
which color fields must be eliminated in order to create a usable print.

Pica: Typesetting measure equal to 12 points, or 1/6'.

PICT: A standard format for object-oriented graphics.

Pixel:
A tiny picture element that contains red, green, and blue information for color rendering
on a monitor or a scanner. When generating colors, pixels are similar to dots of ink on paper.
A monitor resolution description in terms of pixels-per-inch (ppi) is similar
to a printer resolution description in terms of dots per inch (dpi).

PMS:
The Pantone Matching System, an international standard coding system
for colors that lets you specify and match them by number.

PMT:
See Diffusion Transfer - Paper, high contrast - used for live shots, no gray tones.
Paper, half-tone - See halftone - At BPS, available screens 50, 65, 85, 100, 150 lines per inch.
- Film, high contrast - line shots, no gray tones.

Point:
A typesetting measure equal to about 1/72". Font size and amount of leading is typically measured in points.

Portrait:
The normal way pages are printed, with the shorter sides at the top and bottom. Compare landscape.

PostScript:
A programming language developed by Adobe that's specifically designed to handle text and graphics
and their placement on a page. Used primarily in laser printers and image-setters.

PostScript Font: An outline font that works with PostScript.

Printer Driver: File that tells the computer how to send information to a particular kind of printer.

Printer Font:
An outline- font designed to be used by printer (not on the screen.)
Printer fonts are normally represented on the screen by bit-mapped screen fonts.

Resident Font: An outline font that is stored in a printer's ROM, so it is always available for printing.

Reverse Reading:
Letters reading from right to left instead of left to right as observed in a mirror.
Such letters will be right- reading looking through the back of a transparent medium.
Intermediates are usually run reverse-reading, so the image is closest to the receiving print, limiting distortion.

Right Reading: A copy which is readable from the front or image side of the material.

Registration Marks:
Marks used to insure that individual prints will line up correctly when making a composite.

Reproducible:
Capable of producing copies in diazo or photographic methods.

RIP: Raster Image Processor, converts files from vector format (drawing) to raster format (printing).

RTF (Rich Text Format):
A format for transferring files between applications while retaining text and formatting information.

Sans Serif: A font that has no hooks or curls added to the basic shape of the characters.

Scalable Font: Another name for an outline font.

Screen Font:
The bit-mapped version of printer font, used to represent the font on the computer's screen.

Separation Negatives:
Negatives made through appropriate filters so that each carries densities
representing the value of one of the primary colors used in color printing.

Sepia Intermediates: Printing intermediates made on diazo intermediate paper or film.

Serif:
A little hook or line added to the basic form of a character to make a font more readable or decorative.
Compare Sans Serif.

Silkscreen Process:
A stencil method of printing through a piece of silk or other fine cloth on
which all parts of the design not to be printed have been stopped out by an impermeable substance.

Sintra Board: A plastic mounting substrate which can be shaped/bent.

Slick: See clear film positive.

Tab-delimited:
Database programs and files that follow the Mac standard of using tabs to separate fields.

Text File: An ASCII file - just characters, no formatting.

32-Bit Addressing:
A feature of Mac CIS 7 that enables the Mac to use more than eight megabytes of RAM.

TIFF (Tagged Image File Format):
A standard graphics format for high-resolution (72+ dpi) bit-mapped images, like those from most scanners.

Tough Tare White Mylar:
A photograde, weatherproof, opaque white presentation film used
in the d-print process on the Oce 9800; see d-print.

Transparency:
Monochrome or color positive, the image to be viewed or reproduced by transmitted light.

TrueType:
Outline font developed by Apple and supported by Mac OS 7.
Unlike PostScript fonts, a single TrueType font f film le works for both screen and any printer.

24-Bit Color:
Images where each pixel has 24 bits of memory assigned to it,
which produces a palette of 16.7 million colors or shades of gray.

Type 1 Font:
PostScript font format, developed by Adobe Systems, that incorporates hinting, a way of making
the characters of an outline font look as good as possible when printed at low resolutions.

Type 3 Font:
A PostScript font format that is well-suited for complex,
graphic-oriented fonts (characters that contain shades of gray, for example).

WYSIWYG (WIZ-ee-wig):
What you see is what you get - which means that the image
on the computer screen is what will print on paper.

Wash off Mylar:
erasable 4 mil mylar photographic film with drafting surface (matte) on two sides.

Wire-0:
binding system using 32 small square punched holes and a double looped wire spine
which allows a document to open 360 degrees.
