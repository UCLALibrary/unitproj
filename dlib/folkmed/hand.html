Wayland Hand was born in 1907 and died in route to the American Folklore Society in 1986. He was born in New Zealand and was a scholar of German by training. After teaching briefly at the University of Minnesota, Hand joined the faculty at UCLA. True to its disciplinary origins the Folklore and Mythology Program and Archive at UCLA came out of the study of Germanic languages and literatures.
In 1939, Wayland Hand taught the first introductory class ever offered in folklore at UCLA. He was instrumental, along with folk song scholar D. K. Wilgus, in founding the Folklore and Mythology Program at UCLA. Hand was respected as both a scholar and a teacher. He achieved much in his life, serving as founder and editor of the journal Western Folklore, presiding over the American Folklore Society, winning the Pitré Prize for a published work on folklore of international significance, and being elected a Fellow of the American Folklore Society as well as knighted in Finland.

In perhaps no other endeavor was Hand as tireless and unceasing as he was in developing the Folk Medicine Archive. After helping establish folklore studies at UCLA, he devoted the rest of his life to collecting, archiving, and writing about folk beliefs and medical practices.
